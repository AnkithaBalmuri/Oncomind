# 14. Cancer Staging and Grading

## Overview

Cancer staging and grading are critical components of cancer evaluation. After a cancer diagnosis is confirmed, healthcare professionals determine:

* **Stage**: How much cancer is present and how far it has spread.
* **Grade**: How abnormal the cancer cells look and how aggressively they are likely to grow.

Together, staging and grading help doctors:

* Select appropriate treatments
* Predict prognosis
* Estimate survival outcomes
* Compare treatment results
* Monitor disease progression

---

# What is Cancer Staging?

Cancer staging describes the extent of cancer within the body.

### Purpose of Staging

* Determine tumor size
* Assess local invasion
* Identify lymph node involvement
* Detect distant metastasis
* Guide treatment planning

---

# Why Staging is Important

Accurate staging helps:

### Treatment Selection

Different stages require different treatments.

Examples:

* Early-stage cancers may be treated with surgery alone.
* Advanced cancers often require multiple treatments.

---

### Prognosis Estimation

Earlier stages generally have better outcomes.

---

### Clinical Research

Allows comparison of treatment effectiveness among patients.

---

# The TNM Staging System

The most widely used cancer staging system is the TNM system.

Developed by the American Joint Committee on Cancer and the Union for International Cancer Control.

---

# T = Primary Tumor

Describes the size and extent of the main tumor.

---

## TX

Primary tumor cannot be assessed.

---

## T0

No evidence of primary tumor.

---

## Tis

Carcinoma in situ.

Cancer cells remain confined to their original location.

---

## T1

Small localized tumor.

---

## T2

Larger tumor but still relatively localized.

---

## T3

Tumor has grown into nearby tissues.

---

## T4

Tumor extensively invades surrounding structures.

---

# N = Regional Lymph Nodes

Describes whether cancer has spread to nearby lymph nodes.

---

## NX

Lymph nodes cannot be assessed.

---

## N0

No lymph node involvement.

---

## N1

Cancer present in a small number of nearby lymph nodes.

---

## N2

More extensive lymph node involvement.

---

## N3

Significant lymph node spread.

---

# M = Distant Metastasis

Describes spread to distant organs.

---

## M0

No distant metastasis.

---

## M1

Cancer has spread to distant sites.

Common metastatic locations:

* Liver
* Lungs
* Brain
* Bones

---

# Stage Grouping

TNM values are combined into overall stages.

---

# Stage 0

## Carcinoma In Situ

Characteristics:

* Abnormal cells present
* No invasion into nearby tissues

### Examples

* Cervical intraepithelial neoplasia
* Ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS)

### Prognosis

Excellent when treated.

---

# Stage I

## Early Localized Cancer

Characteristics:

* Small tumor
* No lymph node involvement
* No metastasis

### Treatment

Often treated with surgery or localized therapy.

### Prognosis

Generally excellent.

---

# Stage II

## Localized but Larger Cancer

Characteristics:

* Larger tumor size
* Limited local spread
* Possible early lymph node involvement

### Treatment

May require surgery and additional therapies.

---

# Stage III

## Regional Spread

Characteristics:

* Significant local invasion
* Lymph node involvement
* No distant metastasis

### Treatment

Usually requires multimodal treatment.

Examples:

* Surgery
* Radiation
* Chemotherapy

---

# Stage IV

## Metastatic Cancer

Characteristics:

* Cancer spread to distant organs

Common sites:

* Liver
* Lungs
* Brain
* Bones

### Treatment Goals

* Disease control
* Symptom management
* Prolonged survival

---

# Examples of Stage IV Disease

### Breast Cancer

Spread to:

* Bones
* Liver
* Lungs

---

### Lung Cancer

Spread to:

* Brain
* Liver
* Bones

---

### Colorectal Cancer

Spread to:

* Liver
* Lungs

---

# Clinical vs Pathological Staging

---

## Clinical Staging (cTNM)

Based on:

* Physical examination
* Imaging studies
* Laboratory tests

Performed before treatment.

---

## Pathological Staging (pTNM)

Based on:

* Surgical findings
* Tissue examination

Usually more accurate.

---

# What is Cancer Grading?

Cancer grading describes how abnormal cancer cells appear under a microscope.

It reflects:

* Degree of differentiation
* Growth rate
* Aggressiveness

---

# Differentiation

Differentiation describes how closely cancer cells resemble normal cells.

---

## Well-Differentiated Cells

Look similar to normal cells.

Characteristics:

* Slower growth
* Less aggressive

---

## Poorly Differentiated Cells

Look very abnormal.

Characteristics:

* Faster growth
* More aggressive behavior

---

# Cancer Grades

---

## Grade 1 (Low Grade)

### Characteristics

* Well differentiated
* Similar to normal cells

### Behavior

* Slow growth
* Lower risk of spread

### Prognosis

Usually favorable.

---

## Grade 2 (Intermediate Grade)

### Characteristics

* Moderately differentiated

### Behavior

* Moderate growth rate

---

## Grade 3 (High Grade)

### Characteristics

* Poorly differentiated

### Behavior

* Faster growth
* Increased metastatic potential

---

## Grade 4 (Undifferentiated)

### Characteristics

* Highly abnormal cells

### Behavior

* Very aggressive
* Rapid growth

---

# Importance of Grading

Grading helps predict:

* Tumor behavior
* Recurrence risk
* Treatment response

Higher-grade cancers generally require more aggressive treatment.

---

# Cancer-Specific Grading Systems

Some cancers use specialized grading systems.

---

# Gleason Score (Prostate Cancer)

Measures prostate cancer aggressiveness.

### Range

6–10

### Interpretation

| Score | Risk Level        |
| ----- | ----------------- |
| 6     | Low Risk          |
| 7     | Intermediate Risk |
| 8–10  | High Risk         |

---

# Nottingham Grade (Breast Cancer)

Evaluates:

* Tubule formation
* Nuclear appearance
* Mitotic activity

Used to classify breast cancer aggressiveness.

---

# FIGO Staging

Used for:

* Cervical cancer
* Ovarian cancer
* Endometrial cancer

Developed by the International Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics.

---

# Ann Arbor Staging System

Used primarily for:

* Hodgkin lymphoma
* Non-Hodgkin lymphoma

Stages:

* I
* II
* III
* IV

---

# Factors Influencing Prognosis

Staging and grading are important, but other factors matter too.

---

## Tumor Size

Larger tumors generally have worse outcomes.

---

## Lymph Node Involvement

Associated with increased recurrence risk.

---

## Metastasis

Strong predictor of prognosis.

---

## Molecular Characteristics

Examples:

* HER2 status
* EGFR mutations
* BRAF mutations

---

## Patient Factors

* Age
* General health
* Immune status

---

# Restaging

Cancer may be reassessed after treatment.

Purpose:

* Evaluate treatment response
* Detect progression
* Plan additional therapy

---

# Limitations of Staging and Grading

## Tumor Heterogeneity

Different parts of the tumor may behave differently.

---

## Evolving Disease

Cancer characteristics can change over time.

---

## Rare Cancers

May not fit standard staging systems.

---

# Emerging Advances

## Molecular Staging

Uses genetic and molecular information.

---

## Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Improves interpretation of pathology and imaging.

---

## Precision Oncology

Combines stage, grade, and molecular features for personalized treatment.

---

# Relationship Between Stage and Grade

| Feature    | Stage              | Grade                  |
| ---------- | ------------------ | ---------------------- |
| Measures   | Extent of spread   | Cell appearance        |
| Determines | Where cancer is    | How aggressive it is   |
| Based On   | TNM system         | Microscopic evaluation |
| Importance | Treatment planning | Prognosis prediction   |

---

# Key Takeaways

* **Cancer stage** describes the size and spread of cancer.
* The **TNM system** evaluates tumor size (T), lymph node involvement (N), and metastasis (M).
* Stages range from **Stage 0 (in situ)** to **Stage IV (metastatic disease)**.
* **Cancer grade** describes how abnormal cancer cells appear under a microscope.
* Grades range from **Grade 1 (low grade)** to **Grade 4 (high grade)**.
* Higher stages and grades generally indicate more aggressive disease and may require more intensive treatment.
* Specialized systems such as the **Gleason Score**, **FIGO Staging**, and **Ann Arbor Staging** are used for specific cancers.
* Staging and grading together provide essential information for treatment planning and prognosis.

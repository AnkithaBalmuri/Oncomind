# 5. Colorectal Cancer

## Overview

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is a type of cancer that begins in the colon or rectum, which are parts of the large intestine. It is one of the most common cancers worldwide and a leading cause of cancer-related deaths. Most colorectal cancers develop slowly over many years and often start as non-cancerous growths called **polyps**.

Early detection through screening can identify precancerous polyps and significantly reduce the risk of developing colorectal cancer.

---

# What is Colorectal Cancer?

Colorectal cancer occurs when abnormal cells in the colon or rectum grow uncontrollably and form a tumor.

### Colon

The colon is the longest part of the large intestine and absorbs water and nutrients from digested food.

### Rectum

The rectum is the final section of the large intestine that stores stool before it leaves the body.

Cancer can develop in either area and may spread to nearby tissues, lymph nodes, or distant organs if not treated.

---

# Anatomy of the Large Intestine

The large intestine consists of:

### Cecum

Beginning of the colon.

### Ascending Colon

Moves upward on the right side of the abdomen.

### Transverse Colon

Crosses the abdomen.

### Descending Colon

Moves downward on the left side.

### Sigmoid Colon

Connects the colon to the rectum.

### Rectum

Stores waste before elimination.

### Anus

Exit point of the digestive tract.

---

# Types of Colorectal Cancer

## 1. Adenocarcinoma

### Overview

Accounts for approximately 95% of colorectal cancers.

### Origin

Develops from mucus-producing gland cells lining the colon and rectum.

---

## 2. Gastrointestinal Stromal Tumors (GIST)

Rare tumors that develop from specialized cells in the digestive tract wall.

---

## 3. Lymphoma

Cancer of immune cells that can occasionally occur in the colon.

---

## 4. Carcinoid (Neuroendocrine) Tumors

Develop from hormone-producing cells in the intestine.

---

## 5. Sarcoma

Rare cancers arising from connective tissues such as muscles and blood vessels.

---

# How Colorectal Cancer Develops

Most colorectal cancers follow a progression known as the **adenoma-carcinoma sequence**.

### Step 1: Normal Colon Tissue

Healthy cells line the colon wall.

### Step 2: Polyp Formation

Small growths called polyps develop.

### Step 3: Advanced Polyp

Genetic mutations accumulate.

### Step 4: Cancer Formation

The polyp becomes cancerous.

### Step 5: Invasion and Metastasis

Cancer spreads into deeper tissues and distant organs.

This process often takes 10–15 years.

---

# Types of Polyps

## Adenomatous Polyps (Adenomas)

Most likely to become cancerous.

---

## Sessile Serrated Polyps

Can also progress to cancer.

---

## Hyperplastic Polyps

Usually non-cancerous and low risk.

---

# Risk Factors

## Age

Risk increases significantly after age 45.

---

## Family History

Higher risk if close relatives have colorectal cancer or polyps.

---

## Genetic Syndromes

Examples:

* Lynch Syndrome
* Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)

---

## Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Long-term inflammation increases risk.

Examples:

* Ulcerative Colitis
* Crohn's Disease

---

## Diet

Higher risk associated with:

* Red meat consumption
* Processed meats
* Low-fiber diets

---

## Obesity

Excess body weight increases risk.

---

## Physical Inactivity

Sedentary lifestyle contributes to risk.

---

## Smoking

Long-term tobacco use increases risk.

---

## Alcohol Consumption

Heavy alcohol use is associated with higher risk.

---

# Signs and Symptoms

Early-stage colorectal cancer may not cause symptoms.

### Common Symptoms

#### Change in Bowel Habits

* Diarrhea
* Constipation
* Narrow stools

---

#### Blood in Stool

May appear:

* Bright red
* Dark red
* Black

---

#### Rectal Bleeding

Persistent bleeding requires medical evaluation.

---

#### Abdominal Pain

Cramping or discomfort.

---

#### Unexplained Weight Loss

A common warning sign of many cancers.

---

#### Fatigue

Often due to anemia caused by chronic bleeding.

---

#### Feeling That the Bowel Does Not Empty Completely

Persistent sensation after bowel movements.

---

# Screening and Early Detection

Screening is highly effective because it can detect precancerous polyps before cancer develops.

---

## Colonoscopy

### Overview

The gold standard screening method.

### Advantages

* Direct visualization of the colon.
* Allows removal of polyps.
* Can obtain biopsies.

---

## Stool-Based Tests

### Fecal Immunochemical Test (FIT)

Detects hidden blood in stool.

---

### Stool DNA Test

Detects abnormal DNA from cancer cells.

---

## Flexible Sigmoidoscopy

Examines the lower portion of the colon.

---

## CT Colonography

Virtual colonoscopy using CT imaging.

---

# Diagnosis

## Medical History and Physical Examination

Assessment of symptoms and risk factors.

---

## Colonoscopy

Most important diagnostic procedure.

---

## Biopsy

Tissue samples are examined under a microscope.

A biopsy is required to confirm cancer.

---

## Blood Tests

May include:

### Complete Blood Count (CBC)

Detects anemia.

### Carcinoembryonic Antigen (CEA)

A tumor marker used for monitoring treatment.

---

## Imaging Tests

### CT Scan

Evaluates spread to nearby organs.

---

### MRI

Particularly useful for rectal cancer staging.

---

### PET Scan

Detects metastatic disease.

---

# Staging of Colorectal Cancer

## Stage 0

Cancer limited to the innermost lining.

---

## Stage I

Cancer has grown into deeper layers but not beyond the colon wall.

---

## Stage II

Cancer extends through the colon wall.

---

## Stage III

Cancer has spread to nearby lymph nodes.

---

## Stage IV

Cancer has spread to distant organs.

Common metastatic sites include:

* Liver
* Lungs
* Peritoneum

---

# TNM Staging System

### T (Tumor)

Extent of primary tumor invasion.

### N (Nodes)

Number of affected lymph nodes.

### M (Metastasis)

Presence of distant spread.

---

# Treatment Options

Treatment depends on:

* Tumor location
* Stage
* Patient health
* Molecular characteristics

---

## Surgery

Primary treatment for most localized cancers.

### Polypectomy

Removal of cancerous polyps.

---

### Colectomy

Removal of part of the colon.

---

### Proctectomy

Removal of part or all of the rectum.

---

## Chemotherapy

Commonly used for:

* Stage III disease
* Stage IV disease
* High-risk Stage II disease

Common drugs include:

* Fluorouracil
* Capecitabine
* Oxaliplatin

---

## Radiation Therapy

Primarily used for rectal cancer.

### Purposes

* Shrink tumors before surgery
* Reduce recurrence risk
* Palliate symptoms

---

## Targeted Therapy

Targets specific molecular pathways.

Examples include:

* Bevacizumab
* Cetuximab

---

## Immunotherapy

Used in selected patients with specific genetic features.

Examples include:

* Pembrolizumab
* Nivolumab

---

# Side Effects of Treatment

## Surgery

* Infection
* Bleeding
* Temporary bowel changes

---

## Chemotherapy

* Fatigue
* Nausea
* Diarrhea
* Neuropathy

---

## Radiation Therapy

* Skin irritation
* Bowel irritation
* Fatigue

---

# Prevention

## Healthy Diet

Increase:

* Fruits
* Vegetables
* Whole grains

Reduce:

* Processed meats
* Excess red meat

---

## Regular Physical Activity

Helps lower risk.

---

## Weight Management

Maintaining a healthy weight is protective.

---

## Avoid Tobacco

Smoking increases colorectal cancer risk.

---

## Limit Alcohol

Moderation is recommended.

---

## Routine Screening

One of the most effective preventive measures.

---

# Prognosis

Prognosis depends on:

* Stage at diagnosis
* Tumor biology
* Treatment response

### Early-Stage Disease

Often curable with surgery.

### Advanced Disease

May require combined treatment approaches but can still be managed effectively.

---

# Living with Colorectal Cancer

Supportive care may include:

### Nutritional Counseling

Managing diet after surgery or treatment.

### Physical Rehabilitation

Improving strength and recovery.

### Psychological Support

Managing anxiety and emotional stress.

### Survivorship Programs

Long-term monitoring and follow-up care.

---

# Key Takeaways

* Colorectal cancer begins in the colon or rectum and is one of the most common cancers worldwide.
* Most cases arise from precancerous polyps over many years.
* Risk factors include age, genetics, obesity, smoking, alcohol use, and poor diet.
* Symptoms include blood in stool, bowel habit changes, abdominal pain, and unexplained weight loss.
* Colonoscopy is the most effective screening and diagnostic tool.
* Treatment may involve surgery, chemotherapy, radiation therapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy.
* Early detection greatly improves survival and can prevent cancer through removal of precancerous polyps.
* Regular screening and healthy lifestyle habits are key strategies for prevention.

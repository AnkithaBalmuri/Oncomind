# 3. Breast Cancer

## Overview

Breast cancer is a disease in which abnormal cells in the breast grow uncontrollably and form a tumor. It is one of the most common cancers worldwide and the most frequently diagnosed cancer among women. Although it is much more common in women, men can also develop breast cancer.

Early detection through screening and awareness significantly improves treatment outcomes and survival rates.

---

# What is Breast Cancer?

The breast is made up of:

* Lobules (milk-producing glands)
* Ducts (tubes that carry milk to the nipple)
* Fatty tissue
* Connective tissue
* Blood vessels
* Lymphatic vessels

Breast cancer most commonly begins in:

### Ductal Cells

Cancer that starts in the milk ducts is called **Ductal Carcinoma**.

### Lobular Cells

Cancer that starts in the milk-producing glands is called **Lobular Carcinoma**.

Cancer can remain confined to its original location or spread to nearby tissues and distant organs through the blood and lymphatic systems.

---

# Anatomy of the Breast

The breast contains:

### Lobules

Produce breast milk.

### Ducts

Transport milk to the nipple.

### Nipple

Opening through which milk exits.

### Areola

Pigmented area surrounding the nipple.

### Lymph Nodes

Help fight infections and are important pathways for cancer spread.

Common lymph node locations:

* Axillary (underarm)
* Internal mammary
* Supraclavicular

---

# Types of Breast Cancer

## 1. Ductal Carcinoma In Situ (DCIS)

### Definition

A non-invasive cancer confined to the milk ducts.

### Characteristics

* Early-stage cancer
* Has not spread outside ducts
* Highly treatable

---

## 2. Invasive Ductal Carcinoma (IDC)

### Definition

Cancer that begins in the ducts and spreads into surrounding breast tissue.

### Characteristics

* Most common type
* Accounts for approximately 70–80% of cases

---

## 3. Invasive Lobular Carcinoma (ILC)

### Definition

Cancer originating in lobules that invades surrounding tissue.

### Characteristics

* Second most common type
* May affect both breasts

---

## 4. Triple-Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC)

### Definition

Cancer cells lack:

* Estrogen receptors (ER)
* Progesterone receptors (PR)
* HER2 protein

### Characteristics

* More aggressive
* Fewer targeted treatment options
* More common in younger women

---

## 5. HER2-Positive Breast Cancer

### Definition

Cancer cells produce excessive HER2 protein.

### Characteristics

* Rapid growth
* Responds to HER2-targeted therapies

---

## 6. Inflammatory Breast Cancer

### Definition

A rare and aggressive form.

### Symptoms

* Redness
* Swelling
* Warmth
* Skin thickening

---

## 7. Male Breast Cancer

### Definition

Breast cancer occurring in men.

### Characteristics

* Rare
* Usually diagnosed later due to low awareness

---

# Risk Factors

## Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

### Gender

Women are at much higher risk.

### Age

Risk increases with age.

### Family History

Risk increases if close relatives have had breast cancer.

### Genetic Mutations

Important inherited mutations:

* BRCA1
* BRCA2
* PALB2

### Personal History

Previous breast cancer increases future risk.

### Dense Breast Tissue

Makes cancer harder to detect and slightly increases risk.

---

## Modifiable Risk Factors

### Obesity

Especially after menopause.

### Physical Inactivity

Lack of exercise increases risk.

### Alcohol Consumption

Risk rises with increasing alcohol intake.

### Smoking

May contribute to increased risk.

### Hormone Replacement Therapy

Long-term use can increase risk.

---

# Signs and Symptoms

## Early Symptoms

Many patients have no symptoms initially.

---

## Common Symptoms

### Breast Lump

Most common symptom.

Characteristics:

* Usually painless
* Hard or irregular

---

### Breast Swelling

Part or all of the breast may swell.

---

### Skin Changes

Examples:

* Dimpling
* Thickening
* Orange-peel appearance (Peau d’orange)

---

### Nipple Changes

* Inversion
* Retraction
* Crusting

---

### Nipple Discharge

Especially bloody discharge.

---

### Underarm Lump

May indicate lymph node involvement.

---

# Causes and Development

Breast cancer develops due to DNA mutations that cause cells to grow uncontrollably.

Mutations may result from:

* Aging
* Environmental factors
* Hormonal influences
* Inherited genetic defects

Over time:

1. Normal cells mutate.
2. Abnormal cells multiply.
3. Tumor forms.
4. Cancer invades nearby tissue.
5. Metastasis may occur.

---

# Breast Cancer Screening

## Mammography

### Definition

Low-dose X-ray examination of the breast.

### Benefits

* Detects cancer before symptoms appear.
* Reduces mortality.

---

## Clinical Breast Examination

Performed by healthcare professionals.

---

## Breast Self-Awareness

Individuals should be familiar with the normal appearance and feel of their breasts.

---

## Breast MRI

Used for:

* High-risk individuals
* Dense breast tissue
* Additional evaluation

---

# Diagnosis

## Medical History and Physical Examination

Assessment of symptoms and risk factors.

---

## Imaging Tests

### Mammogram

Most common imaging test.

### Ultrasound

Useful for evaluating lumps.

### MRI

Provides detailed imaging.

---

## Biopsy

### Definition

Removal of tissue for laboratory analysis.

### Types

* Fine-needle aspiration
* Core needle biopsy
* Surgical biopsy

Biopsy is the definitive diagnostic method.

---

# Breast Cancer Staging

Staging describes the extent of disease.

## Stage 0

* Non-invasive cancer (DCIS)

---

## Stage I

* Small tumor
* No significant lymph node involvement

---

## Stage II

* Larger tumor and/or limited lymph node spread

---

## Stage III

* Extensive local spread
* Multiple lymph nodes involved

---

## Stage IV

* Metastatic disease
* Spread to distant organs

Common metastatic sites:

* Bone
* Liver
* Lung
* Brain

---

# TNM Staging System

### T (Tumor)

Size and extent of primary tumor.

### N (Nodes)

Lymph node involvement.

### M (Metastasis)

Presence of distant spread.

---

# Treatment Options

Treatment depends on:

* Cancer type
* Stage
* Receptor status
* Patient health

---

## Surgery

### Lumpectomy

Removes tumor while preserving breast tissue.

### Mastectomy

Removes the entire breast.

---

## Radiation Therapy

Uses high-energy radiation to destroy cancer cells.

Often used after surgery.

---

## Chemotherapy

Uses drugs to kill rapidly dividing cancer cells.

May be given:

* Before surgery (neoadjuvant)
* After surgery (adjuvant)

---

## Hormone Therapy

Used for hormone receptor-positive cancers.

Common medications:

* Tamoxifen
* Anastrozole
* Letrozole

---

## Targeted Therapy

Used for HER2-positive cancers.

Examples:

* Trastuzumab
* Pertuzumab

---

## Immunotherapy

May be used in selected triple-negative breast cancers.

---

# Side Effects of Treatment

## Surgery

* Pain
* Scarring
* Limited arm movement

---

## Chemotherapy

* Hair loss
* Nausea
* Fatigue
* Increased infection risk

---

## Radiation

* Skin irritation
* Fatigue

---

## Hormone Therapy

* Hot flashes
* Joint pain
* Mood changes

---

# Prevention

Although not all cases can be prevented, risk can be reduced by:

* Maintaining a healthy weight
* Regular exercise
* Limiting alcohol consumption
* Avoiding smoking
* Breastfeeding when possible
* Participating in screening programs

---

# Prognosis and Survival

Prognosis depends on:

* Stage at diagnosis
* Tumor biology
* Response to treatment

### Early-Stage Breast Cancer

Often highly treatable with excellent survival rates.

### Metastatic Breast Cancer

Usually not curable but can often be managed for extended periods with modern therapies.

---

# Living with Breast Cancer

Patients may benefit from:

* Nutritional counseling
* Physical activity programs
* Psychological support
* Support groups
* Survivorship care plans

---

# Key Takeaways

* Breast cancer is one of the most common cancers worldwide.
* It usually begins in the ducts or lobules of the breast.
* Risk factors include age, genetics, hormones, obesity, and lifestyle factors.
* Common symptoms include breast lumps, nipple changes, and skin abnormalities.
* Mammography is the primary screening tool.
* Diagnosis is confirmed through biopsy.
* Treatment may involve surgery, radiation, chemotherapy, hormone therapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy.
* Early detection greatly improves outcomes.
* Awareness, screening, and timely treatment are essential for reducing breast cancer mortality.
